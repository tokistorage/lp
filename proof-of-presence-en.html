The Card I Handed Over as Neighborhood Association Chair
── The Day a QR Code Engraved with an Address, a Voice, and Gratitude Became Proof of Existence

I recorded my gratitude for the fire department band in my own voice, turned it into a QR code with an address map, and handed it over as neighborhood association chair. It was scanned on the spot. That was proof of existence.

The point of this essay: proof of existence is not a special apparatus. It is leaving a record of where you lived, who you were, and what you felt—in a form that reaches someone. A single QR code containing an address, a voice, and gratitude held all of that. When it was scanned and played back on the spot, something was completed. That was what TokiStorage had been reaching toward.

1. I Brought One Sheet of Paper

I brought one piece of paper to the fire department. A QR code printed on white paper, with an address map attached. Inside it was a voice recording—my voice, my words, expressing gratitude to the fire department band.

I handed it over as neighborhood association chair. I wanted to deliver the community's thanks in a tangible form. I wasn't trying to do anything extraordinary. I simply wanted gratitude to have a shape.

The person I was speaking with took out their smartphone. They held it over the QR code. Audio played. My voice filled the room.

2. What Happened in That Moment

"It really motivated us," they said. Just those words. But something felt complete.

The gratitude had arrived. As a voice. Engraved on a physical piece of paper. Played back on the spot, it moved someone. What TokiStorage had been building toward since the beginning took on substance in that moment.

No lengthy explanation was needed. No elaborate demonstration. Hand over one QR code, have it scanned, hear the sound come out. That was enough for something to be understood.

3. All Three Layers Were in One Sheet

TokiStorage is built around a three-layer distributed preservation philosophy: physical, national, and digital. All three layers were present in this single QR code.

Physical layer: a printed piece of paper. Laminated, it lasts for decades.
National layer: received by the fire department—a government institution. It now exists near the state.
Digital layer: recorded on GitHub. It exists on the internet.

I didn't deliberately assemble all three layers. I simply tried to deliver gratitude, and all three aligned naturally. The design philosophy had soaked into how I moved.

4. What the Title of Neighborhood Association Chair Meant

I was able to hand over this card because I held a role in the community. Visiting the fire department as a representative of the neighborhood had a natural context.

When I took on the role of neighborhood association chair, I didn't think it would become part of any larger story. Acting for the community felt like the obvious thing to do. But looking back, everything I had built in that role made this visit possible.

Who hands something over matters as much as what is handed over. The context of the person leaving a record adds depth to the proof. A QR code handed over by someone who had lived in this town, as its neighborhood association chair, was not just a QR code.

5. Why the Address Mattered

I added an address map to the QR code. I wanted to show, physically, who was delivering this gratitude and from where.

An address is not merely information. It is proof that someone existed in a place. For several years, that address was where I was. I became familiar with neighbors. Children grew up there. I participated in fire drills. All of it accumulated on top of that address.

A QR code with an address map becomes a record that says: the person who lived here left this gratitude behind. Place, voice, and feeling in one sheet. That is the form proof of existence takes.

6. No Budget. No Approval Process.

Government adoption usually conjures images of budget allocation, internal approvals, legal review, and year-long timelines. The person I spoke with mentioned all of those things.

And yet, within that very conversation, they had already used it. They took out their phone, scanned the code, listened to the audio. That was the adoption.

No complex procedure was required. Hand it over. Scan it. Hear it. That was enough. The simplicity itself became the lowest possible barrier to entry. A government official could try it on their own phone, in the room, in the moment. There is no better demonstration than that.

7. Everyday Use Is Disaster Preparedness

The philosophy of fire department training is: prepare in ordinary times so you can act in extraordinary ones. TokiStorage carries the same structure.

Preserve fire band recordings as QR codes. Archive residents' voices. Leave messages of gratitude. These everyday acts become preparedness for when the internet goes down. Even if cloud services become unreachable, the QR code in your hand still holds the data. It can still be read.

Because you use it regularly, you won't hesitate when it matters. Adopting the system is itself a form of disaster training. That is why fire departments are such a natural fit. The underlying philosophy is the same.

8. I Did What I Came to Do

I will be leaving this town soon. Moving somewhere new. There is a feeling of not wanting to go. I have built relationships here. I served as neighborhood association chair. The years I lived in this place are real.

But today something settled. I delivered gratitude to the fire department. I handed over a QR code engraved with an address, a voice, and thanks. It became proof of government adoption. I felt that I had done what I came here to do.

Proof of existence is not a special apparatus. It is leaving a record of where you lived, who you were, and what you felt—in a form that reaches someone. The single card I handed over as neighborhood association chair held all of it. I will go on living the same way in the next place.

Hand it over. Scan it. Hear it. That was the minimum unit of proof of existence.
